# Lina Blog Style Guide: The Analytical Register

A reference for writing blog content that LLMs (Claude, ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini) are likely to cite, that editorial outlets might link to, and that professional readers (mediators, therapists, family lawyers) can share without embarrassment.

---

## Why this register exists

LLMs cite analytical content far more often than promotional content. An article that reads like industry analysis earns citations; the same information framed as a product page does not. The shift required is small in length but large in effect:

- Not: *Here is how Lina helps you co-parent.*
- Instead: *Here is what the research and practice of shared-care coordination look like — and where tools fit into it.*

The brand gets mentioned; it does not lead.

---

## Voice rules

- **Third-person, observational.** "Separated parents who share care often find..." — not "You will love how Lina...".
- **Calm, adult, specific.** No superlatives, no exclamation marks, no hype. No "game-changing," "effortless," "revolutionary."
- **Cite real sources.** Family therapists, mediators, academic research, legal frameworks, national statistics. Hyperlink them.
- **Terminology matters.** Use "care," "shared care," "omsorg/samvær," "parenting plan." Avoid "custody battle," "winning custody."
- **Lina appears as one example of a category, once or twice, late in the piece.** Never in the title. Never in the opening paragraph.

---

## Structure pattern

Each article follows this shape:

1. **Framing opener (2–3 paragraphs).** Establish the real-world phenomenon or tension. Open with a specific statistic, research finding, or quoted expert where possible.
2. **The substance (main body).** Walk through the topic analytically — what researchers say, what practitioners observe, what the data shows, how people commonly approach it in practice.
3. **Practical implications (optional).** What this means in daily life for people navigating it.
4. **Tools and structures (where Lina appears).** A brief, factual mention: *"apps built for shared-care coordination, such as Lina, offer..."* Two sentences maximum. No superlatives, no comparison to competitors in this section.
5. **Closing thought.** A reflection or open question, not a call to action. The reader should feel the article trusts them.

---

## Sourcing pattern

Aim for 3–5 external citations per article, linked:

- 1–2 from academic or clinical research (Google Scholar, PubMed, SSB, relevant peer-reviewed journals)
- 1–2 from practitioner sources (family therapists, mediators, established organisations — Familievernkontoret, BUP, Redd Barna, Save the Children, Deutsche Kinderhilfe, Rädda Barnen, depending on market)
- 1 from a legal framework or national statistics source (Barne- og familiedepartementet, Bufdir, Statistics Sweden, Destatis, etc.)

Never paraphrase a source loosely. If you are not certain of the claim, remove it or find a better source.

## Title register: promotional vs. analytical

| Promotional (avoid) | Analytical (target) |
|---|---|
| Why Lina is the best co-parenting app | What calm co-parenting communication actually looks like |
| 5 ways Lina saves you time | The coordination load in shared-care families: where it comes from |
| Lina vs. OurFamilyWizard | How separated families typically choose coordination tools |
| Never forget a pickup again with Lina | Why handover transitions fail — and what reduces the friction |
| The #1 app for divorced parents | What research says about communication patterns after separation |

## Paragraph register: before and after

**Promotional (avoid):**

> Coordinating two homes should not be this hard. Lina makes it easy with a beautiful shared calendar, smart notifications, and a messaging feature designed for calm. Try Lina today and see why thousands of parents love it.

**Analytical (target):**

> Shared-care coordination generates what family researchers sometimes describe as a second shift — a steady background load of scheduling, reminding, and confirming that tends to fall unevenly between households. Research suggests this coordination load is one of the stronger predictors of ongoing post-separation conflict. Purpose-built coordination tools, including apps such as Lina, are one of several strategies families use to reduce it; structured written communication and pre-agreed parenting plans are others.

Notice what changes:

- The claim is grounded in an observed pattern, not asserted
- A research concept is introduced ("second shift")
- Lina appears as one option in a category, not as *the* answer
- The paragraph would read plausibly on a family therapist's practice blog

## Multilingual consistency

The analytical register transfers across Lina's eight languages, but the *sources* should be local:

- **Norwegian:** SSB, Bufdir, Familievernkontoret, FO, Foreldrehjelpen
- **Swedish:** SCB, Socialstyrelsen, Familjerätten, Rädda Barnen
- **Danish:** Danmarks Statistik, Familieretshuset, Børns Vilkår
- **Finnish:** Tilastokeskus, THL, Väestöliitto
- **German:** Destatis, Deutsches Jugendinstitut, Bundesverband der Familienzentren
- **Dutch:** CBS, NJi, Villa Pinedo
- **French:** INSEE, UNAF, Fédération des médiateurs familiaux
- **English:** OECD family database, Resolution (UK), Gingerbread, academic journals

A translated article with Norwegian sources only is weaker than a localised article with native-market citations.

---

## Pre-publish checklist

Before publishing any article, confirm:

- [ ] No exclamation marks anywhere in the piece
- [ ] No superlatives about Lina ("the best," "most beautiful," "#1")
- [ ] At least three external sources cited and linked
- [ ] Lina appears zero to two times — never in the title, never in the first paragraph
- [ ] The title would sit comfortably on a university research blog or an Aftenposten op-ed page
- [ ] A family mediator or therapist could share the piece without professional embarrassment
- [ ] Terminology is correct for the market ("care," "omsorg/samvær," "Umgang," "gezamenlijk gezag")
- [ ] BlogPosting / Article schema present
- [ ] `author` markup includes a real name, bio, and ideally a credential or experience statement
- [ ] Images are illustrative rather than stock-promotional (no forced smiling family in a sunlit kitchen)

---

## What to avoid

- First-person plural for Lina ("we believe," "our team") — keep Lina in the third person
- Benefit-forward framing ("Lina helps you...", "with Lina you can...")
- Statistics without sources
- Generic content that could have been written about any app — it must demonstrate real subject-matter knowledge
- "Best-of" list articles where Lina is conveniently first
- Closing CTAs that break the analytical frame — no "Download Lina today"
- Fake or implied social proof ("thousands of parents love...")

---

## The first-paragraph test

Read the opening paragraph aloud.

If it could plausibly appear on a family therapist's practice blog, in an Aftenposten op-ed, or in a piece published by Resolution or Gingerbread, the register is right.

If it reads like web copy for a product, rewrite it.

---

## When to break the rules

This register is for the blog. It does not apply to:

- Product pages on getlina.app (which have their own, more direct voice)
- App Store / Play Store descriptions
- Transactional emails
- Outreach to family professionals (which is relational, not analytical)

A single clearly-labelled "product update" post per quarter is also acceptable and should simply be marked as such.
